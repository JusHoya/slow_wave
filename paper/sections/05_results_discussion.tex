\subsection{Primary endpoint}
On the preregistered primary endpoint --- the paired (by-seed) difference in
final ACC between \texttt{full\_dream} and \texttt{no\_sleep} in the
\SWprimaryRegime{} regime --- the verdict is \textbf{\SWprimaryVerdict}. The mean
paired difference is $\Delta=\SWprimaryDiff$ (bootstrap 95\% CI
$[\SWprimaryCIlo,\SWprimaryCIhi]$, \SWprimaryCImethod{} method), the paired
\SWprimaryTest{} test gives $p=\SWprimaryP$, and the standardised paired effect
is Cohen's $d=\SWprimaryD$ (\SWprimaryEffectMag). The effect exceeds the A/A
noise floor of \SWnoiseFloor{} (exceeded=\SWexceedsNoiseFloor) --- not a fixed
threshold but the empirically measured gap between the mean final ACC of the
reference config (\texttt{no\_sleep}) evaluated over two disjoint seed families, a
single cross-seed mean-difference point --- so it clears the bar a seed-only
difference would produce. Within the mock regime, however, these inferential
quantities describe dispersion across seed-driven stream re-instantiations rather
than inference about a real model: the summariser is deterministic, so there is
zero model-side sampling variance (the stability control's token CV is
\SWstabilityCV, identical=\SWstabilityIdentical). The \SWprimaryTest{} $p$ is
moreover near-saturated at $n=\SWnSeeds$ seeds --- $p=\SWprimaryP$ is essentially
the smallest two-sided value the test can return once all \SWnSeeds{} per-seed
differences share a sign, which is why it equals the secondary contrast's
$p=\SWdreamVsReplayP$ --- so we read it descriptively and defer genuine
significance testing to a real-model run. The same direction holds in all three
regimes: $\Delta=\SWdiffSignalRich$ (signal-rich, \SWverdictSignalRich),
$\SWdiffBalanced$ (balanced, \SWverdictBalanced), and $\SWdiffDistractorHeavy$
(distractor-heavy, \SWverdictDistractorHeavy). Within the mock-LLM regime, then,
the full dream cycle's operators behave as designed: consolidation protects
signal --- it preferentially evicts distractors, retaining a far larger fraction
of true signal than the no-sleep baseline (the bench's signal-retention metric,
quantified in the next subsection) --- and transfers it into the semantic store so
it survives episodic eviction, lifting final accuracy over the no-sleep reference. This is a signal-retention effect, not
a higher aggregate prune F1 (on which the ground-truth-blind control actually
scores higher), as the next subsection unpacks, and it is bought at higher token
and memory cost than the no-sleep reference (Table~\ref{tab:arms},
Figure~\ref{fig:pareto}), not at matched budget. This preregistered primary effect
is measured against the no-sleep baseline --- and the registered secondary
contrast against the replay-only control --- the ablation arms that isolate the
mechanism; it is not a claim that the full cycle is the best deployable arm. A
simpler deployable control (\texttt{reflection}) reaches higher final accuracy at
lower token cost, which we surface below as a first-class cost finding, on equal
footing with the long-context comparison.

The per-task retention pattern (Figure~\ref{fig:retention}) shows the mechanism:
the no-sleep baseline retains only the most recent tasks (strongly negative
backward transfer in Table~\ref{tab:arms}), whereas replay-bearing arms retain
earlier-task signal. The replay\,$\times$\,downscale $2\times2$ ablation
(Figure~\ref{fig:ablation}) isolates the contribution of each mechanism, and the
registered secondary contrast confirms that the full cycle also beats
replay-only ($\Delta=\SWdreamVsReplayDelta$, 95\% CI
$[\SWdreamVsReplayCIlo,\SWdreamVsReplayCIhi]$, Wilcoxon $p=\SWdreamVsReplayP$),
again at higher token and memory cost than that re-exposure-only control
(Table~\ref{tab:arms}) --- i.e.\ in this regime the effect is not merely
re-exposure.

\subsection{Consolidation quality, decoupled from accuracy}
Because the bench knows ground truth offline, it scores pruning directly
(Figure~\ref{fig:mechanism}). The \texttt{oracle} arm attains the highest prune
precision/recall (it prunes exactly the injected distractors), as it must ---
a sanity ceiling. Critically, prune quality and downstream accuracy can diverge:
in Table~\ref{tab:arms} the highest-accuracy arm (\texttt{long\_context}) does no
pruning at all, and the most precise pruner is not the most accurate arm --- which
is exactly why the bench reports the two \emph{decoupled} rather than collapsing
them into a single score.

The starkest illustration is the ground-truth-blind \texttt{random\_pruning}
control: in Table~\ref{tab:arms} it posts the \emph{highest} non-oracle prune F1,
yet the \emph{lowest} accuracy of any arm. That F1 is an artifact of aggressive,
indiscriminate pruning --- it removes most distractors only because it removes
almost everything (high recall at base-rate precision), discarding signal in the
same sweep and collapsing downstream accuracy. The full cycle shows the opposite
profile: high prune \emph{precision} (most of what it evicts is genuine
distractor, not signal) at lower recall (it leaves many distractors in place), so
its aggregate F1 sits below the blind control even as its accuracy is far higher. Measured directly, the full
cycle keeps a far larger fraction of true \texttt{signal} in active memory than
either the no-sleep baseline or the random control --- the bench's
signal-retention complement to prune recall, and the signal-protecting retention
the mechanism predicts --- so the accuracy gain tracks \emph{which} material is
kept and transferred to the durable store, not a higher summary F1. An arm can win
the F1 score and still lose the task, which is exactly why the bench reports prune
quality and accuracy as two numbers rather than one.

\subsection{Cost: the long-context and deployable-control comparisons (first-class negatives)}
The accuracy-vs-compute frontier (Figure~\ref{fig:pareto}) and the stream-length
sweep (Figure~\ref{fig:crossover}) carry the most important caveat to the
positive primary result. The \texttt{long\_context} baseline --- stuffing the
entire stream into context with no consolidation --- attains the highest raw
accuracy at the highest memory footprint (Table~\ref{tab:arms}); in this mock
regime its realised \emph{token} cost is the lowest tier, because answers come
from exact-key lookup rather than model inference over the stuffed context --- an
artifact a real long-context model would not share, as it would pay a token cost
growing with stream length. On the cost-adjusted metric
(\SWcrossoverMetric{}, \SWcrossoverTreatment{} vs \SWcrossoverBaseline{}) there
is \textbf{no crossover} in the swept range $L\in\{\SWcrossoverLengths\}$
(crossover found=\SWcrossoverFound): the long-context baseline keeps its
raw-accuracy lead across all tested lengths. We state plainly why this holds in
the present regime: probe answers are read by exact-key lookup over active memory,
never from the model (Appendix~\ref{app:hyper}), and \texttt{long\_context} keeps
every item (unbounded episodic capacity), so it answers every probed key and its
perfect final accuracy in Table~\ref{tab:arms} is a \emph{tautology of the design}
rather than a demonstration of long-context reasoning. The absence of a
cost-adjusted crossover in $L\in\{\SWcrossoverLengths\}$ is therefore structurally
entailed here, not empirically uncovered: a real long-context model would pay
tokens that grow with $L$ and would risk the lost-in-the-middle degradation that
exact-key lookup cannot exhibit, either of which could pull a crossover into the
swept range. This is precisely one of our
preregistered negative-result forms --- ``long-context wins below stream length
$L$'' --- and we report it as a first-class finding, not a footnote: within the
lengths we could afford to sweep, external consolidation does \emph{not} beat
stuffing the stream into a long context on raw accuracy, and whether a crossover
exists at larger $L$ is an open, explicitly unanswered question. The
contribution here is to \emph{map the cost regime}: consolidation's advantage in
this bench is a memory-footprint advantage (the consolidated arms hold fewer
vectors than \texttt{long\_context} in Table~\ref{tab:arms}, the full cycle at
higher token cost), not a matched-budget result and not a raw-accuracy win over an
unbounded context.

\paragraph{The treatment is not the best deployable arm.} The same frontier
carries a second, equally first-class cost finding. \texttt{reflection} --- the
deployable shallow-synthesis control of \citet{park2023generativeagents},
\emph{not} a ceiling like \texttt{oracle} or \texttt{long\_context} --- attains
higher final accuracy than \texttt{full\_dream} at lower token cost and comparable
memory footprint (Table~\ref{tab:arms}). On the accuracy-versus-token objective of
the reported Pareto frontier (maximise ACC, minimise tokens;
Figure~\ref{fig:pareto}), \texttt{full\_dream} is therefore Pareto-dominated by
\emph{both} \texttt{long\_context} and \texttt{reflection} and sits off the
non-dominated frontier. We state this with the same prominence as the long-context
result: the full cycle's confirmed advantage is over the no-sleep and replay-only
\emph{ablation} arms --- the mechanism question H1 was written to answer --- not
over the strongest deployable comparator. The picture is a genuine trade-off
rather than uniform inferiority: on the mechanism-level prune-quality readout the
full cycle retains higher recall and F1 than \texttt{reflection}
(Table~\ref{tab:arms}), and what the four-operator cycle alone provides is
operator-level \emph{decomposability} (each operator independently ablatable,
Figure~\ref{fig:ablation}) and the consolidation-quality measurement --- not a
Pareto-superior accuracy-per-token operating point. \MockCaveat

\subsection{TMR-style targeting}
Within the mock-LLM regime the replay operator does what it was built to do:
replay-bearing arms show a mean signal-retention lift of $\SWtmrLift$ over
non-replay arms (Figure~\ref{fig:tmr}) --- the targeting analogue measurably moves
signal into the durable store, which is the informative content of this contrast.
Standardised, the lift is a bias-corrected Hedges' $g=\SWtmrG$ (95\% CI
$[\SWtmrCIlo,\SWtmrCIhi]$), against a meta-analytic sleep-TMR benchmark of
$g\approx\SWtmrBench$ \citep{hu2020tmr}. We deliberately do \emph{not} read the
margin between the two as ``beating'' that benchmark, for two reasons. First, this
is a prioritized-replay \emph{targeting analogue}, not a literal cued-TMR protocol,
so any comparison is to a benchmark effect size, not a claim of biological
equivalence. Second, and more fundamentally, the standardised magnitude arises from
the same synthetic-mock determinism behind the very large primary $d=\SWprimaryD$
(Section~\ref{sec:limitations}): a deterministic operator engineered to reinject
labelled signal will out-score a benchmark drawn from noisy human data almost by
construction. Under the mock, then, the benchmark comparison cannot, even in
principle, be meaningfully favourable or unfavourable; what \emph{is} informative
is the sign of the lift and the demonstration that the operator moves signal as
designed.

\subsection{Sim-vs-real agreement, power, and bias controls}
Time-compression does not invert the arm ranking (Figure~\ref{fig:simreal}):
across key arms the accelerated high-compression sim and the real
low-compression long-horizon runs agree with Pearson $\SWsimPearson$ and
Spearman $\SWsimSpearman$ (\SWsimInversions{} inversions), with a maximum
absolute mean-ACC divergence of $\SWsimMaxDiv$. The ranking is preserved across
the compared arms, but a perfect Spearman over only a few, widely-separated arms
is weak evidence on its own, and that divergence is itself non-negligible; we
therefore read this check as \emph{suggestive} that time-compression does not
invert the coarse ordering here, not as establishing the accelerated sim as a
validated proxy --- the divergence is reported rather than hidden, and we revisit
its limits in Section~\ref{sec:limitations}. For \emph{power}, the realised
$n=\SWpowerN$ seeds clears the floor of \SWpowerFloor; at the observed effect
($d=\SWpowerObsD$) a paired design requires only $n\ge\SWpowerReqN$ seeds
\citep{colas2018seeds}, so the design is amply powered \emph{for an effect this
large} --- a caveat we revisit in Section~\ref{sec:limitations}, since such a
large $d$ is itself an artifact of the deterministic mock regime. The bias
controls are clean: the temperature-0 stability control finds the mock
summariser identical across repeats (token CV $=\SWstabilityCV$,
identical=\SWstabilityIdentical), and the memory-drift detector reports
faithfulness $\SWdriftFaithfulness$ below the degradation threshold
$\SWdriftThreshold$ (degraded=\SWdriftDegraded).

\paragraph{Interpretation.} In the mock-LLM mechanism-demonstration regime, the
apparatus does what it was built to do: each operator is independently
controllable, consolidation provably moves signal into a durable store, prune
quality is measured against ground truth and is decoupled from accuracy, and the
honest cost picture (long-context dominance on raw accuracy) is surfaced rather
than buried. What these results do \emph{not} establish --- and cannot, under a
mock LLM --- is that a real Claude model would show the same effect, or that the
effect would survive a real summariser's nondeterminism and drift. That is the
experiment the bench is built to run next.
